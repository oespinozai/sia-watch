SIA Licensing in the UK: A Complete Guide for Security Operators

If you work in the private security industry in the UK, an SIA licence is not optional. It is a legal requirement, and the rules around obtaining, renewing, and maintaining that licence are stricter than many operators realise. Whether you are an individual door supervisor keeping tabs on your own renewal, or a security company managing a roster of fifty operatives, understanding how SIA licensing works is fundamental to staying on the right side of the law.

This guide covers everything you need to know: what the SIA is and why it exists, the different licence types and what each covers, how to apply, how to renew, the refresher training requirements that changed in April 2026, what happens when a licence lapses, and the most common compliance mistakes security companies make when managing licences at scale.

What Is SIA Licensing and Why Does It Exist?

The Security Industry Authority (SIA) is the UK government body responsible for regulating the private security industry. It was established under the Private Security Industry Act 2001, which created a mandatory licensing regime for individuals working in designated sectors of the security industry. The goal was straightforward: to raise standards, weed out those with serious criminal records, and give the public confidence that the people protecting venues, events, and individuals had been vetted and trained to a consistent standard.

Before the SIA existed, the private security industry was largely unregulated. Employers could hire anyone for front-line security roles regardless of criminal history or training. The 2001 Act changed that. Today, it is a criminal offence to work in a licensable role without a valid SIA licence. Both the operative and any employer who knowingly deploys an unlicensed operative can face prosecution.

SIA licensing UK covers a defined list of activities. If your role involves one of those activities, you need the appropriate licence. There is no grey area on this point. The SIA publishes a clear list of what counts as a licensable activity, and ignorance of that list is not a legal defence.

The SIA public licence register is searchable by name or licence number at sia.homeoffice.gov.uk. Anyone can check whether a licence is current, expired, or revoked. This is the same check your employer is legally obligated to perform before deploying you.

Licence Types: What Each One Covers

The SIA issues licences across several distinct sectors. Each licence authorises the holder to work only in the activities it covers. Holding a security guard licence, for instance, does not permit you to work as a door supervisor. You must hold the correct licence for the role.

Door Supervisor Licence

The door supervisor licence is the most widely held SIA licence in the UK. It covers individuals who work on the doors of licensed premises such as pubs, clubs, and bars, as well as those providing physical security or crowd management at events. Door supervisors are also permitted to carry out vehicle immobilisation under certain conditions.

To qualify for a door supervisor licence, applicants must hold a Level 2 Award for Door Supervisors (or the equivalent qualification that was in place before the current framework). The qualification covers physical intervention, conflict management, fire safety awareness, first aid, and the law as it applies to the role. Door supervisor licence renewal is required every three years.

Security Guard Licence

The security guard licence covers static guarding roles. If you are protecting premises, assets, or property rather than managing access to a licensed venue, this is the licence you need. Security guards working in retail loss prevention, guarding construction sites overnight, or protecting commercial premises all fall into this category.

The required qualification is a Level 2 Award for Security Guards, which covers patrolling, surveillance, access control, and emergency procedures. The security guard licence does not permit the holder to work in door supervision roles, and vice versa.

Close Protection Licence

Close protection operatives, sometimes called bodyguards, require a close protection licence. This is the most demanding licence to obtain and the one with the most significant recent changes to renewal requirements. Close protection compliance has been an area of particular regulatory focus by the SIA in recent years.

Applicants must hold a Level 3 Award in Close Protection (Ofqual-regulated) and meet additional criteria around first aid qualifications, driving, and in some cases firearms awareness. The close protection sector attracts a wide range of operatives, from those protecting high-net-worth individuals and corporate executives to those working in hostile environments overseas on UK contracts.

CCTV (Public Space Surveillance) Licence

Operators who monitor CCTV cameras in public spaces require an SIA licence. This covers individuals working in control rooms where CCTV is used to monitor public areas, typically for local authorities, transport operators, or large venues. Monitoring CCTV on private premises for internal security purposes does not require an SIA licence, but the line between public and private space surveillance is one that catches operators out regularly.

The required qualification is a Level 2 Award for CCTV Operators (Public Space Surveillance).

Cash and Valuables in Transit Licence

Individuals who transport cash or high-value goods require a cash and valuables in transit licence. This covers cash-in-transit couriers, those collecting from ATMs, and operatives working with high-value asset transportation. This is a specialist sector with its own qualification pathway and its own set of compliance risks, particularly around vehicle security and route planning.

Vehicle Immobiliser Licence

Wheel clamping on private land requires a vehicle immobiliser licence. This is a comparatively small sector, and the rules around where immobilisation is lawful are heavily regulated under the Protection of Freedoms Act 2012. Clamping on public roads remains illegal regardless of licence status.

Key Holding Licence

Key holders who respond to alarms and hold keys to premises on behalf of clients require a key holding licence. This is often held in combination with a security guard licence.

How to Apply for an SIA Licence

New applications are made online through the SIA website. The process is managed entirely digitally, and the SIA no longer accepts paper applications. Before you begin, you need to have your required qualification in place. You cannot apply for a licence and complete your training simultaneously; the SIA needs to see proof of qualification before they will process the application.

The application process involves the following steps:

  1. Complete the required training and obtain your qualification certificate from an SIA-approved awarding body.
  2. Create an account on the SIA's online licensing portal.
  3. Submit your application including personal details, qualification reference, and identity documents.
  4. Pay the licence fee. The current fee is £190 per licence.
  5. Consent to a criminal records check. The SIA checks all applicants against the Police National Computer and, where applicable, the Disclosure and Barring Service (DBS). The depth of check varies by licence type.
  6. Wait for a decision. The SIA states a target of 25 working days, but complex cases involving criminal history or identity verification issues can take longer.

The SIA applies a fit and proper person test to all applicants. Certain convictions will result in automatic refusal; others are considered on a case-by-case basis depending on the nature of the offence, the sentence received, and how much time has passed. The SIA publishes a detailed list of the types of criminal history that will prevent licensing, which any applicant with a criminal record should review carefully before paying the application fee.

The SIA Licence Renewal Process and Timeline

SIA licence renewal is not automatic. You must actively apply to renew before your current licence expires. The SIA allows you to apply up to six months before your expiry date, and they strongly recommend applying at least eight weeks before expiry to allow time for processing.

The renewal process mirrors the initial application in many respects. You apply through the online portal, pay the £190 fee, and consent to a further criminal records check. Any changes in your criminal history since your last application will be assessed as part of the renewal.

There is a common misconception that renewing early means you lose time on your new licence. This is not the case. If you renew before your current licence expires, your new three-year term starts from the day after the old one ends, not from the date you submitted the renewal. You do not lose any of the remaining time on your current licence by applying early.

SIA Licence Renewal Timeline: What to Expect

Planning your renewal around the SIA's processing times is essential, particularly for operatives in high-demand roles who cannot afford any gap in coverage. Here is a practical timeline to work from:

  • 6 months before expiry: Earliest point at which you can submit a renewal application. Good time to confirm you have met any refresher training requirements.
  • 3 months before expiry: Recommended start point if your circumstances are straightforward. Gives ample buffer for processing delays.
  • 8 weeks before expiry: SIA's own minimum recommendation. Cutting it this close is inadvisable unless circumstances prevent earlier action.
  • Expiry date: If your renewal has not been approved by this date, you must stop working in the licensable role immediately.

The SIA does not send automatic reminder letters before your licence expires. Tracking your own SIA licence expiry date is your responsibility. Many operatives miss their renewal window because they assumed they would be reminded. They were not.

Refresher Training Requirements

One of the most significant recent changes to SIA licensing relates to refresher training requirements for close protection operatives. Understanding which licence types require additional training at renewal is essential for private security compliance.

Close Protection: April 2026 Change

From April 2026, close protection operatives must complete a Level 4 Award in Close Protection refresher course before they can renew their licence. This is a significant change from previous renewal requirements and has caught some experienced operatives off guard.

The refresher training covers updated threat assessment methodologies, changes in relevant legislation, advances in protective equipment and surveillance countermeasures, and current best practice in close protection planning and advance work. The course is assessed by an Ofqual-regulated awarding body and the certificate must be in place before a renewal application can be submitted.

Close protection operatives whose licences expire from April 2026 onwards will need to factor this training into their renewal timeline. A Level 4 refresher course typically takes several days to complete, and places on accredited courses are limited. If you wait until the last two months before expiry to book your training, you may find yourself unable to secure a place in time.

Close protection compliance is now a two-stage requirement: the refresher training certificate, and then the SIA renewal application itself. Both take time. Budget for at least three to four months of lead time if you fall into this category.

Door Supervisor and Security Guard Renewal

Door supervisors and security guards do not currently need to complete refresher training as a condition of renewal, provided their original qualification is still recognised. The SIA periodically updates qualification frameworks, and applicants with older qualifications may occasionally be required to top up or requalify before they are eligible to renew. Always check the SIA's current guidance on qualification equivalencies when preparing for door supervisor licence renewal.

First Aid Requirements

All front-line licence types require a current, in-date first aid qualification. Most SIA licences require the holder to hold a First Aid at Work certificate or equivalent. These certificates are typically valid for three years, and the renewal dates do not always align with your SIA licence expiry. If your first aid certificate expires before your SIA licence, you may find yourself unable to work even though your licence is technically still valid. Managing both expiry dates separately is one of the more overlooked aspects of individual private security compliance.

What Happens If Your SIA Licence Lapses?

A lapsed SIA licence is not a minor administrative inconvenience. It is a legal problem with real consequences. The moment your licence expires without a valid renewal in place, you cannot legally work in any licensable role. Continuing to work is a criminal offence.

Under the Private Security Industry Act 2001, working without a valid licence carries a maximum penalty of an unlimited fine or up to five years imprisonment, or both. In practice, prosecutions at the custodial end of this range are reserved for cases involving deliberate, persistent, or organised non-compliance, but fines have been issued for straightforward cases of lapsed licensing.

For employers, the stakes are equally serious. A security company that knowingly deploys an unlicensed operative faces the same penalties and also risks losing its Approved Contractor Scheme (ACS) status, which is effectively a commercial death sentence in many parts of the industry. The SIA licence expiry of a single operative, if it is not caught in time, can trigger a compliance audit of the entire operation.

Reinstating a Lapsed Licence

There is no fast-track reinstatement process for lapsed licences. If your licence has expired, you must go through the full application process again as though you were applying for the first time. This means paying the full application fee, consenting to a fresh criminal records check, and waiting the full processing time. If the SIA has introduced any changes to qualification requirements since you last applied, you may also need to complete additional training before your application will be accepted.

The practical consequence is a potential gap of several weeks or longer during which you cannot work in a licensable role. For sole traders and freelance security operatives, this can mean significant lost income. For employers, it means scrambling to find cover at short notice.

There is a narrow grace period concept that sometimes causes confusion. If you submitted your renewal application before your expiry date and the SIA has not yet made a decision, you can continue working while the application is being processed. This only applies if the renewal was submitted before the licence expired. A late application grants no such protection.

Managing SIA Licences Across a Security Team

For individual operatives, keeping track of a single licence expiry date is manageable with a phone calendar reminder. For security companies managing teams of ten, fifty, or hundreds of operatives, the compliance picture is significantly more complex.

A typical security company might have operatives holding door supervisor licences, security guard licences, and close protection licences simultaneously, with staggered expiry dates across the entire roster. Some operatives hold dual licences. New hires join mid-year with licence dates that do not align with anyone else. First aid certificates have their own separate renewal cycle. Operatives on fixed-term contracts may let their licences lapse if they do not expect to be working during the renewal window, then return to work and need emergency reinstatement.

Without a structured system for SIA licence tracking, compliance holes are inevitable. The most common approach at smaller companies is a shared spreadsheet, which works until it does not: a cell is overwritten, a date is entered incorrectly, the person who maintained it leaves the company, or nobody notices when a date rolls past. An SIA licence tracker that automates expiry monitoring and sends proactive reminders removes these failure modes.

What a Compliant Licence Management System Looks Like

A robust approach to managing private security compliance across a team should include:

  • A central record of every operative's licence number, sector, and expiry date, updated at onboarding and whenever a renewal is completed.
  • Automated reminders at 90, 60, and 30 days before each licence expiry, sent to both the operative and a compliance manager.
  • Separate tracking for first aid and any sector-specific training expiry dates that do not align with the licence itself.
  • A clear policy that no operative is deployed to a licensable role without the compliance manager confirming their licence is current and valid.
  • A process for verifying licence status directly against the SIA public register before deployment, not just against internal records.
  • Documentation showing that the above checks were carried out, retained for audit purposes.

The SIA's Approved Contractor Scheme audits companies on exactly these points. If you cannot demonstrate a systematic approach to licence management, you are at risk regardless of whether your actual operative compliance rate is high.

Common Compliance Mistakes Security Companies Make

After working with security operators across the UK, a set of recurring compliance failures comes up time and again. Most of them are avoidable with better systems and clearer processes.

Relying on Operatives to Self-Report

Many companies ask operatives to notify their manager when a licence is coming up for renewal. This puts the compliance burden on the individual operative, who may be busy, distracted, or simply not thinking about it until it is too late. Self-reporting is a supplement to systematic tracking, not a substitute for it.

Not Verifying Against the SIA Register

Maintaining internal records of licence expiry dates is necessary but not sufficient. An operative's licence can be revoked by the SIA between renewals if they receive a qualifying conviction. The only way to know whether a licence is currently valid is to check the SIA public register. Internal records cannot tell you about a mid-cycle revocation.

Treating All Licence Types the Same

The refresher training requirements, qualification frameworks, and application complexity vary significantly between licence sectors. A renewal process that works smoothly for door supervisor licence renewal may be inadequate for close protection compliance, particularly given the April 2026 changes. Each sector needs to be tracked against its own requirements.

Missing the First Aid Certificate Cycle

First aid certificates and SIA licences are renewed on separate cycles, and their expiry dates rarely align. A security company that tracks licence expiry dates but not first aid certificate dates will eventually deploy an operative who is technically licensed but whose first aid qualification is out of date. This is a compliance failure that SIA auditors will identify.

Inadequate Onboarding Checks

New hires sometimes arrive with licences that are closer to expiry than their paperwork suggests. A licence that was issued three years ago and expires in four months is not a problem for the immediate deployment, but it will become one very quickly. Every onboarding process should capture the exact expiry date and trigger the same renewal workflow that applies to existing staff.

Assuming the SIA Will Notify You

The SIA does not proactively remind licence holders or employers that a licence is approaching expiry. Some operatives receive a renewal notice by post, but this is not guaranteed and should not be relied upon. The SIA's position is that managing licence expiry is the holder's responsibility. Companies that build their compliance process on the assumption of receiving an external reminder are building on sand.

SIA Licensing and the Approved Contractor Scheme

The Approved Contractor Scheme (ACS) is a voluntary accreditation scheme run by the SIA that recognises security companies that meet a higher standard of operation. ACS status is effectively required to compete for contracts with larger clients, local authorities, and many public sector organisations. Maintaining ACS status requires demonstrating consistent compliance with SIA licensing requirements across the entire workforce, and ACS audits specifically look at how licence management is handled operationally.

Companies that want to achieve or retain ACS status need to be able to show documented evidence of systematic licence tracking, verification against the SIA register, and proactive renewal management. Ad-hoc spreadsheet systems rarely satisfy auditors at this level. An SIA licence tracker that provides an audit trail of checks and reminders is a material advantage in an ACS assessment.

Staying Current: How SIA Requirements Change

SIA licensing requirements are not static. The April 2026 close protection refresher training requirement is the most significant recent change, but the SIA regularly reviews and updates qualification frameworks, application processes, and sector-specific requirements. Changes are announced on the SIA website and through the industry press, but there is no guarantee that every operative or manager will see every update.

Building a compliance process that checks for regulatory updates as a standing item, rather than relying on individuals to notice changes, is the professional approach. The SIA also publishes consultation documents when significant changes are proposed, giving the industry advance notice of upcoming requirements. Engaging with these consultations, or at minimum reading the outcomes, ensures you are not caught off guard by the next change after the close protection update.

Summary: Key Points for SIA Licensing Compliance

  • All individuals working in licensable security roles in the UK must hold a current, valid SIA licence for that specific role.
  • SIA licences are valid for three years and must be actively renewed. The SIA does not automatically renew licences.
  • Apply for SIA licence renewal at least eight weeks before expiry; three months is safer and six months is the earliest permitted.
  • From April 2026, close protection operatives must complete a Level 4 refresher course before renewing their licence.
  • Working with a lapsed SIA licence is a criminal offence. There is no grace period unless a renewal was submitted before expiry.
  • Employers are legally responsible for verifying that every operative they deploy holds a current licence.
  • First aid certificate expiry dates must be tracked separately from SIA licence expiry dates.
  • The SIA public register allows anyone to verify a licence's current status by name or licence number.
  • Systematic SIA licence tracking across a team requires dedicated processes or tooling, not a passive reliance on operatives to self-report.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does an SIA licence last?

An SIA licence is valid for three years from the date of issue. You should apply for renewal no later than eight weeks before the expiry date to avoid any gap in your ability to work legally.

What happens if my SIA licence expires?

If your licence expires you cannot legally work in a licensable role. Continuing to work without a valid SIA licence is a criminal offence under the Private Security Industry Act 2001, punishable by an unlimited fine or up to five years imprisonment. You must stop working in the role immediately and apply for a new licence, which means going through the full application process again rather than a simple renewal.

Do I need refresher training to renew my SIA licence?

It depends on your licence sector. Door supervisors and security guards do not currently need to complete refresher training to renew, provided their training qualifications are still considered current by the SIA. However, from April 2026, close protection operatives are required to complete a Level 4 Award in Close Protection refresher course before they can renew their licence. The SIA regularly reviews requirements so you should always check the current guidance on the SIA website before applying.

What is the SIA licence renewal fee?

The current SIA licence fee is £190 per licence. This fee applies to both new applications and renewals. Some operatives hold licences in multiple sectors and must pay the fee for each licence separately.

Can an employer apply for an SIA licence on behalf of an employee?

No. An SIA licence is held by the individual, not the employer. Each operative must apply for and renew their own licence. Employers are responsible for verifying that every operative they deploy holds a current, valid licence before assigning them to a licensable role.

How can I track SIA licence expiry dates across a large security team?

The SIA's public licence register allows you to check the status of any individual licence by name or licence number.
